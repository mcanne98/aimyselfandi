Every major technology shift seems to create an obsession around a single skill. During the early days of the internet, organizations competed on who could build the best website. As cloud computing matured, the conversation shifted toward infrastructure and scalability. When smartphones transformed the way we worked, success appeared to belong to those who could build the next great mobile application. Artificial intelligence has followed a remarkably similar pattern. Today, the conversation is dominated by prompt engineering. We have become fascinated by the words we type into AI, believing that better prompts inevitably lead to better outcomes.

There is certainly truth in that belief. Learning how to communicate clearly with AI is an important skill, just as asking thoughtful questions has always been an important leadership skill. A well written prompt provides direction, removes ambiguity, and helps the model understand what is expected. It deserves its place in every professional's toolkit. However, after thousands of conversations with AI spanning executive leadership, board presentations, software engineering, solution architecture, implementation planning, strategic roadmaps, business writing, and consulting, I have become convinced that prompt engineering is no longer where the greatest opportunity lies.

The organizations and individuals creating the most value with AI are rarely distinguished by writing dramatically better prompts. Instead, they approach the entire interaction differently. Before the first prompt is ever written, they have already invested time defining the problem, understanding the audience, identifying constraints, surfacing assumptions, and determining what success actually looks like. By the time they begin interacting with AI, much of the difficult thinking has already been completed. The prompt itself simply becomes the mechanism that transfers that thinking to the model.

This realization did not arrive overnight. Like many people, my own journey with AI began by searching for the perfect prompt. I collected templates, experimented with different structures, and refined my wording in an effort to produce better responses. Sometimes those changes made a noticeable difference. More often, they produced only incremental improvements. What consistently generated better outcomes was not changing the prompt itself, but changing everything that happened before I wrote it.

Looking back, this makes perfect sense. Experienced professionals have never started important work by immediately creating the deliverable. An executive preparing for a board meeting does not begin by opening presentation software. A software architect does not immediately start drawing diagrams. A consultant does not draft recommendations before understanding the client's business. Each of these disciplines begins with a period of structured thinking. Objectives are clarified, stakeholders are identified, context is gathered, competing priorities are balanced, and assumptions are challenged before any meaningful work begins.

Ironically, many of us abandon that discipline the moment we begin working with AI. We open our favorite model, type a request, receive a response, and then spend the next twenty minutes correcting, refining, redirecting, and explaining what we actually meant. The interaction feels productive because words are constantly being generated, yet much of the conversation is simply recovering from thinking that should have happened before the first prompt was written. We mistake activity for progress, when in reality the model is helping us discover information that already existed in our own minds.

Consider a common executive task such as preparing a board presentation on an organization's AI strategy. Most people begin with a straightforward request: "Create a board presentation about our AI strategy." Within seconds, the model produces a respectable first draft. It includes an overview of AI trends, a few implementation recommendations, perhaps a slide on governance and another on expected return on investment. On the surface, it looks impressive. Yet almost immediately the revisions begin. The presentation needs to be more strategic. The financial implications need greater emphasis. The tone should sound more executive. The recommendations need to reflect the organization's priorities. Risks should be addressed more explicitly. Healthcare examples should replace generic business examples. Gradually, the presentation improves through repeated conversations.

What is actually happening during those revisions is fascinating. The AI is not simply rewriting slides. It is gradually uncovering the information that should have been provided from the beginning. Each follow up prompt reveals another piece of context that existed in the author's mind but never made it into the original request. The model is effectively interviewing its user, discovering the audience, the objectives, the desired tone, the constraints, and the measures of success one clarification at a time. By the end of the conversation, the presentation is often excellent, but arriving there required a surprisingly inefficient path.

This pattern extends far beyond board presentations. The same cycle appears when writing technical documentation, designing software, creating implementation plans, reviewing contracts, developing strategic roadmaps, or drafting customer communications. The first prompt is rarely the real beginning of the work. Instead, it marks the point at which our thinking first becomes visible. Everything that happened before that moment ultimately determines the quality of everything that follows.

That observation fundamentally changed the way I think about AI. Instead of asking how I could write better prompts, I began asking a different question. What if prompting was only one small step inside a much larger thinking process? What if the real opportunity was not prompt engineering at all, but designing a repeatable workflow for thinking with AI? Once I began looking through that lens, patterns started appearing everywhere, and those patterns eventually evolved into a methodology that has transformed the way I approach every significant piece of work.

One of the reasons this pattern goes unnoticed is because most of us never see what is happening beneath the surface of an AI conversation. We experience the interaction as a simple exchange of questions and answers, but underneath every response is a finite amount of computational effort. Understanding a few basic concepts about how AI processes information helps explain why some conversations feel effortless while others seem to wander endlessly before producing something useful. Fortunately, these concepts are far less technical than they first appear, and understanding them changes the way you think about working with AI.

Everything an AI model reads and writes is broken into small units called tokens. Rather than processing complete sentences or paragraphs, the model processes these smaller pieces of text as it works through a conversation. You can think of tokens as the currency of communication between you and the model. Every word you type consumes input tokens. Every sentence the model generates consumes output tokens. Modern reasoning models also consume reasoning tokens, representing the internal thinking the model performs before arriving at its final answer. Finally, all of this takes place within what is known as a context window, the amount of information the model can actively keep in mind while generating its response.

An analogy I often use is that of a meeting room. Imagine you are leading a strategy session with your executive team. Every participant enters the room carrying knowledge, assumptions, documents, and ideas. The discussion progresses because everyone can remember what has already been said, connect it to earlier conversations, and build upon previous decisions. If someone walked into the room halfway through the meeting, they would lack the context necessary to contribute effectively. They might ask questions that have already been answered or make recommendations that no longer fit the direction of the discussion. AI behaves in much the same way. The more relevant context it has available, the better it can reason about the problem in front of it.

This explains why conversations with AI often improve over time. It is not necessarily because the model becomes smarter. Rather, it gradually accumulates the context that should have existed from the beginning. Every clarification you provide expands the model's understanding of the task. Every correction reveals another expectation. Every rewrite uncovers another assumption that had been left unstated. By the fifth or sixth iteration, the model has finally assembled enough information to produce what you were hoping for in the first place.

Returning to our board presentation example illustrates this perfectly. The initial request was deceptively simple: create a board presentation on our AI strategy. On the surface, that appears to be enough information, yet from the model's perspective it is missing almost everything that matters. Who is the audience? Is this a board of directors, an executive committee, or an investment board? What decision are they expected to make? Is the goal to secure funding, communicate progress, or obtain approval for a strategic initiative? Should the presentation emphasize financial return, operational risk, organizational readiness, competitive positioning, or regulatory considerations? None of those questions are answered by the original prompt, so the model fills the gaps with reasonable assumptions. The resulting presentation is not wrong. It is simply generic because the request itself was generic.

As the conversation continues, additional context slowly emerges. You explain that the audience consists of board members with limited technical backgrounds. You mention that the presentation needs to justify additional investment. You ask for healthcare examples instead of manufacturing examples. You request a more executive tone and fewer technical details. Each refinement makes the presentation stronger, but each refinement also consumes additional input, output, and reasoning tokens. The model is no longer creating the presentation. It is discovering the presentation you had already envisioned.

This brings us to an idea that I believe deserves far more attention than it currently receives. Most discussions about AI focus on reducing token consumption. The underlying assumption is that fewer tokens automatically mean greater efficiency. While this may be true from an infrastructure or API cost perspective, it is rarely the right metric for measuring business outcomes. Organizations do not create value by consuming the fewest possible tokens. They create value by producing better decisions, better strategies, better software, and better customer experiences.

That distinction led me to a concept I now refer to as Token Efficiency. I define Token Efficiency as maximizing the business value created for every token consumed. The objective is not to minimize conversation. The objective is to ensure that every interaction moves the work meaningfully closer to the desired outcome. Sometimes that will require more tokens, not fewer. Spending additional context to help an executive make a multimillion dollar investment decision is entirely different from spending additional tokens because the AI is rewriting the same presentation for the seventh time.

I often express this idea using a simple equation:

**Business Value per Token = Quality × Accuracy × Relevance × Action ÷ Total Tokens Consumed**

I like this equation because it shifts the conversation away from cost alone. Quality asks whether the work is genuinely useful rather than merely acceptable. Accuracy measures whether the information can be trusted. Relevance determines how closely the output aligns with the real problem being solved. Action asks perhaps the most important question of all: does this output enable someone to make a decision, complete a task, or move the organization forward? Only after considering these dimensions do we divide by the total number of tokens consumed. Suddenly, token usage becomes part of a much broader measure of value rather than the primary objective itself.

The opposite pattern is Token Maxing, and once you recognize it, you begin seeing it everywhere. Token Maxing occurs when conversations become long chains of corrections that add very little incremental value. The interaction often begins with an underdefined request, followed by a series of increasingly specific instructions. "Make it shorter." "Use a more executive tone." "Focus on healthcare." "Remove the technical jargon." "Add financial metrics." "Actually, let's start over." Each iteration feels like progress because new content is being generated, but much of the conversation exists simply to compensate for thinking that never happened before the first prompt.

What makes Token Maxing particularly deceptive is that it feels productive. There is constant activity. The model is generating pages of content, and the user is actively refining it. Yet when viewed from a broader perspective, much of that effort could have been avoided through a more deliberate approach at the outset. The hidden cost is not only additional tokens. It is fragmented thinking, inconsistent outputs, decision fatigue, and the gradual erosion of confidence in AI itself. Many people conclude that AI produces mediocre work, when in reality the workflow leading into the conversation was never designed to produce excellent work.

This is why I believe the future of AI will be shaped less by better prompting and more by better workflow design. The organizations that consistently generate exceptional results will not necessarily be the ones using the largest models or the newest features. They will be the ones that learn how to provide the right thinking, in the right sequence, before the first prompt is ever written. Once you begin viewing AI through the lens of Token Efficiency instead of Token Maxing, a much more important question naturally emerges. If better outcomes come from better thinking before the prompt, what should that thinking process actually look like?

That question became the turning point in my own journey with AI. Once I stopped searching for better prompts and started examining the thinking that preceded them, I began noticing the same pattern repeated across almost every type of work I performed. Whether I was preparing a board presentation, reviewing an architecture proposal, developing a strategic roadmap, designing an implementation plan, writing technical documentation, or helping a leadership team navigate a complex decision, the quality of the final output was rarely determined by the wording of the prompt. It was determined by the quality of the thinking that happened beforehand.

Returning to our board presentation example makes this particularly easy to see. Imagine two executives who have both been asked to prepare a presentation for the Board on their organization's AI strategy. They have access to the same AI model, the same corporate information, and roughly the same amount of time. Yet the presentations they produce are dramatically different.

The first executive opens an AI assistant and immediately begins writing. "Create a Board presentation on our AI strategy." Within seconds, a polished presentation appears. The executive reviews it, identifies gaps, requests revisions, asks for more financial analysis, changes the tone, requests healthcare examples, adjusts the recommendations, removes several slides, adds new ones, and continues refining until something acceptable emerges. The AI has undoubtedly accelerated the work, but the process has been largely reactive. The thinking evolved through the conversation itself.

The second executive takes a different approach. Before opening AI, they spend several minutes clarifying the objective. They ask themselves what decision the Board is expected to make at the end of the presentation. They identify the concerns that individual Board members are likely to raise. They consider how the organization's strategic priorities should shape the narrative. They think about the financial implications, the operational risks, the implementation challenges, and the level of technical depth appropriate for their audience. By the time they engage with AI, they already possess a clear mental model of the story they want to tell.

The interesting part is that neither executive necessarily writes a dramatically better prompt. In fact, the prompts may appear remarkably similar to an outside observer. The difference lies in everything that the second executive knows before they begin. Their prompt carries significantly more intention because it is supported by structured thinking. AI is no longer helping them discover their own ideas. Instead, it is helping them express ideas that have already been carefully developed.

This distinction is subtle, yet it changes the role AI plays entirely. In the first scenario, AI becomes a collaborator in discovering the problem. In the second, AI becomes a force multiplier for solving a problem that has already been clearly defined. Both approaches can eventually produce a good presentation, but one requires substantially less effort, fewer revisions, and far greater consistency.

The more I reflected on this pattern, the more I realized that we have unintentionally treated AI as if it were a search engine. We open a conversation, ask a question, evaluate the response, and continue searching until we find something satisfactory. While this works reasonably well for simple requests, it breaks down as the complexity of the work increases. Strategic decisions, software architecture, organizational design, implementation planning, and executive communication all require a sequence of reasoning that cannot be compressed into a single prompt.

Experienced professionals already understand this intuitively. Architects rarely begin by drawing boxes and arrows. They first seek to understand the business capabilities the architecture must support. Consultants do not begin by writing recommendations. They begin by understanding the client's objectives, constraints, and operating environment. Product managers do not start with feature lists. They start with customer problems and desired outcomes. Great leaders do not begin difficult conversations by choosing their words. They first become clear about the outcome they are trying to achieve.

In every one of these professions, the work follows a process. The deliverable is simply the final expression of that process.

This observation led me to think differently about AI. Rather than treating prompting as the primary skill, I began viewing it as one stage within a much larger system. The real opportunity was not prompt engineering. It was designing the workflow that surrounds the prompt.

I started referring to this discipline as Workflow Engineering.

Workflow Engineering is the intentional design of the thinking process that leads to high quality interactions with AI. It recognizes that AI performs best when it receives clarity rather than improvisation. Instead of relying on repeated corrections to gradually uncover the objective, Workflow Engineering encourages us to establish that objective before the conversation begins. Context is gathered deliberately rather than accidentally. Assumptions are identified instead of remaining hidden. Plans are developed before content is generated. Reviews become structured evaluations rather than emotional reactions to a first draft.

One of the reasons I find this idea so compelling is that it shifts our attention from the model to the work itself. Much of today's AI discussion revolves around comparing models. Which model reasons better? Which one writes better code? Which one has the largest context window? Which one performs best on benchmarks? Those are interesting questions, but they are increasingly becoming less important. Every major model continues to improve at an extraordinary pace. Capabilities that once differentiated one model quickly become available across the industry.

If the models continue converging, then competitive advantage must move somewhere else.

I believe it moves into the workflow.

The organizations that consistently outperform their competitors will not simply own better AI models. They will design better systems for applying intelligence across the business. They will establish repeatable ways of capturing context, surfacing assumptions, structuring reasoning, reviewing outputs, and turning ideas into action. Their advantage will not come from individual prompts. It will come from institutionalizing how their people think with AI.

This mirrors almost every major transformation we have experienced in business. Competitive advantage rarely comes from possessing a tool that everyone else can eventually acquire. It comes from designing better operating systems around that tool. Lean manufacturing was never about machinery alone. Agile development was never about project management software. Cloud transformation was never simply about moving servers. The technology mattered, but the organizations that benefited most redesigned the way they worked around it.

I believe AI is following the same path.

We are gradually moving beyond the era of asking, "How do I write a better prompt?" and entering an era where a much more valuable question emerges: "How do I design a better workflow for thinking with AI?"

That question became the foundation for everything that followed. Over time, through thousands of real world conversations and countless iterations across leadership, engineering, architecture, consulting, and business communication, those observations gradually crystallized into a repeatable methodology. I did not set out to invent a framework. I simply kept noticing the same sequence of thinking appearing over and over again until it became impossible to ignore. Eventually, I gave that sequence a name: The AI Workflow Framework.

When I first recognized this pattern, I did what many professionals do when something repeatedly proves successful. I started documenting it. Initially, it was nothing more than a collection of notes describing how I prepared different types of work. Over time, however, those notes evolved into a repeatable sequence that I found myself following regardless of the task in front of me. Whether I was preparing an executive presentation, reviewing a software architecture, developing a strategic roadmap, writing technical documentation, planning an implementation, or creating one of these newsletters, the thinking process remained remarkably consistent.

What surprised me most was not that the process worked. It was that it worked across disciplines. Executive leadership, consulting, engineering, architecture, product management, implementation, and technical writing all appeared to follow the same underlying pattern. The deliverables were different, but the thinking behind them was strikingly similar. Once I recognized that, I stopped thinking about AI interactions as isolated conversations and started viewing them as workflows that could be designed, improved, and repeated.

That realization eventually became what I now call The AI Workflow Framework.

## THE AI WORKFLOW FRAMEWORK

```
/goals
      ↓
/context
      ↓
/assumptions
      ↓
/plan
      ↓
/draft
      ↓
/review
      ↓
/iterate
      ↓
/action
```

At first glance, the framework appears deceptively simple, and that is intentional. Every stage represents a question that experienced professionals naturally ask themselves before moving forward. Rather than depending on memory or intuition, the framework makes those questions explicit and repeatable. It transforms what was once an informal thought process into a structured workflow that can be applied consistently across almost any type of knowledge work.

The journey begins with **/goals**. Before asking AI to produce anything, it is worth asking what success actually looks like. What decision needs to be made? What outcome are we trying to influence? Who is the audience? What would make this work genuinely valuable? Defining success first ensures that AI is solving the right problem instead of simply generating content.

Once the destination is clear, **/context** provides the environment in which the work exists. Context includes background information, organizational priorities, stakeholder expectations, constraints, previous decisions, available data, and anything else that helps AI understand the situation. Most disappointing AI outputs are not caused by poor reasoning. They are caused by insufficient context.

The next stage, **/assumptions**, is one that many people skip entirely. Every project contains assumptions, some explicit and others hidden. Identifying them early allows you to challenge them before they become embedded in the final output. It also gives AI the opportunity to identify gaps, raise questions, and expose risks that might otherwise remain invisible.

With those foundations established, **/plan** creates a roadmap for the work itself. Instead of asking AI to immediately generate the final deliverable, the conversation first focuses on structure, sequencing, and approach. This often becomes the highest leverage stage in the entire workflow because a well designed plan dramatically improves the quality and consistency of everything that follows.

Only then does **/draft** enter the picture. By this stage, AI is no longer creating content in isolation. It is building upon clearly defined objectives, rich context, validated assumptions, and a structured plan. The first draft is no longer the beginning of the thinking process. It becomes the first expression of thinking that has already been completed.

The following stages recognize that meaningful work is rarely perfect on the first attempt. **/review** evaluates the draft against the original goals rather than relying on subjective reactions. It asks whether the work is complete, accurate, relevant, persuasive, and aligned with the intended outcome. Instead of reviewing the writing alone, it reviews the thinking behind the writing.

From there, **/iterate** focuses on targeted improvements rather than endless rewriting. This distinction matters. Iteration should increase clarity, strengthen reasoning, and improve quality. It should not become an excuse for repeatedly correcting work that lacked direction from the beginning. When the earlier stages have been completed well, iteration becomes refinement rather than recovery.

Finally, **/action** asks the question that many AI conversations never reach. What happens next? A presentation should lead to a decision. A strategy should influence execution. Documentation should enable implementation. Code should be deployed. Insights should become measurable business outcomes. AI creates the greatest value when its outputs move beyond information and become action.

Prompt engineering taught us how to talk to AI.

Workflow engineering will teach us how to think with AI.
