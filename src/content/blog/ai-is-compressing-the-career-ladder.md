For most of modern business history, careers followed a fairly predictable path.

You started by learning the mechanics of the job. You spent years understanding processes, observing experienced colleagues, and building confidence through repetition. Over time, you accumulated context, developed judgment, and eventually earned the trust to make bigger decisions.

Experience was the bridge between execution and leadership.

Organizations were designed around that progression.

Junior employees executed tasks. Mid-level employees coordinated work. Senior employees provided judgment, coaching, and direction. The ladder was clear, and moving up that ladder required time.

AI is changing that.

When most people talk about AI and the future of work, the conversation quickly focuses on automation. Which jobs will disappear? Which tasks will be replaced? How many people will organizations need?

Those are important questions, but I think they distract us from a much more interesting shift that is already underway.

AI is not simply automating work.

It is accelerating access to expertise.

For the first time, employees early in their careers can access capabilities that previously required years of experience to develop. A junior analyst can create executive-ready presentations. A new project coordinator can receive recommendations on risks and dependencies. An early-career developer can troubleshoot complex problems with guidance that resembles having a senior engineer sitting beside them.

The work has not disappeared.

But the gap between novice and expert has started to narrow.

And that changes expectations.

Historically, organizations hired junior employees with the understanding that they would need time to develop. The expectation was not that they would immediately exercise judgment. The expectation was that they would learn the process, execute consistently, and gradually build the experience necessary to make larger decisions.

AI is quietly rewriting that contract.

When AI can explain the process, generate the first draft, summarize the documentation, identify risks, and provide recommendations, the value of the employee increasingly shifts away from execution and toward judgment.

The question becomes less about whether someone can complete a task and more about whether they can evaluate the output, identify what matters, and make a good decision.

That is a skill we have traditionally associated with senior employees.

Today, it is becoming an expectation much earlier in a person's career.

I have started thinking about this as the seniorization of junior roles.

Not because junior employees suddenly possess decades of experience, but because the role itself increasingly demands behaviors that used to emerge much later. The employee may have only a few years of experience, yet they are being asked to assess recommendations, challenge assumptions, prioritize competing options, and make decisions with real business impact.

In many ways, AI is compressing the career ladder.

Tasks that once occupied the first several years of a career are becoming automated, accelerated, or heavily assisted. As a result, employees are reaching higher-order work much faster than previous generations.

This sounds exciting, and in many ways it is.

But it also creates a challenge.

Because while AI can accelerate access to knowledge, it does not automatically create judgment.

Judgment is still developed through experience. It comes from understanding tradeoffs. It comes from making mistakes. It comes from learning that there is often more than one correct answer and that context matters as much as analysis.

An employee may have access to the same information as a senior leader, but information alone is not what makes someone effective.

The ability to interpret information is what matters.

This is where leadership becomes more important, not less.

Many organizations assume that as AI becomes more capable, the need for coaching will decrease. I suspect the opposite is true.

As employees move more quickly into judgment-oriented work, leaders must spend more time helping them develop the decision-making skills that AI cannot provide. Coaching becomes less about teaching someone how to execute a process and more about helping them understand why one decision is better than another.

The role of management evolves as well.

Managers spend less time reviewing work and more time developing thinking. Less time directing execution and more time providing context. Less time answering questions and more time helping employees ask better questions.

In many ways, AI is seniorizing junior roles while simultaneously forcing managers to become better leaders.

And I think this is one of the most important workforce shifts of the next decade.

For years, organizations have worried that AI would reduce the value of human work.

What if the opposite happens?

What if AI allows employees to contribute at a level that previously took ten years to reach?

What if the real challenge is not replacing workers, but preparing them for responsibilities they will encounter much earlier than anyone expected?

Because if that is true, then the future competitive advantage will not belong to the organizations with the best AI.

It will belong to the organizations that become exceptionally good at developing judgment.

AI can accelerate capability.

It can accelerate learning.

It can accelerate execution.

But judgment remains a deeply human skill.

And as AI continues to compress the career ladder, the ability to develop that skill at scale may become one of the most important leadership responsibilities in the modern workplace.
